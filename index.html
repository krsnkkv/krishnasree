About

I'm a final-year MEng Computer Science & Software Engineering student at the University of Birmingham, finishing my degree at the Edgbaston campus after three years at Birmingham's Dubai campus.

My dissertation — How Do We Hear a Rāga? — is an interpretable ML pipeline for Carnatic rāga classification. It uses pitch contour extraction, tonic normalisation, swara mapping, and transition-based features to classify rāgas the way a trained listener might. The headline result: 67.4% track accuracy using dwell-vadi features with SVM and LSTM classifiers.

Outside the lab, I co-founded Rahla, an AR travel app that reached the App Olympics semi-finals; lead creative and operations at Nova Studio, an indie game studio; and delivered "Can a Machine Dream?", a public workshop on AI and cognition for the UAE National Program for AI.

The long-term thread: I believe cognition should constrain and validate computational design. Music is my entry point — not the destination.

How Do We Hear a Rāga?

My MEng dissertation: a six-stage interpretable ML pipeline for Carnatic rāga classification. Extracts pitch contours with torchcrepe, normalises tonic, maps to swaras, builds transition features, then classifies with SVM and LSTM under GroupKFold cross-validation. Achieved 67.4% track accuracy with dwell-vadi features. Tonic estimation remains the central bottleneck — dropping segment accuracy from ~60% to ~43%.
